Chandigarh

"The City Beautiful — where modernist vision meets Punjabi soul."

Chandigarh is India's first planned city — a UNESCO World Heritage site designed from scratch by Swiss-French architect Le Corbusier after Partition in 1947. Situated at the foot of the Shivalik Hills, it serves as the shared capital of Punjab and Haryana and forms a bustling tricity with Mohali and Panchkula. Known for its extraordinary modernist architecture, 43+ parks and gardens, vibrant café culture, and the warmth of its Punjabi character, Chandigarh ranks consistently as India's most liveable city. It is 260 km northwest of Delhi, 104 km southwest of Shimla, and gateway to the hills of Himachal Pradesh.

01
Origins & Visionaries

The Birth of Chandigarh

When India gained independence in 1947, Partition severed Punjab from its ancient capital Lahore. Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru saw in this loss an extraordinary opportunity — to build from scratch a capital that would signal India's modernity. "Let it be a new town," he declared, "symbolic of the freedom of India, unfettered by the traditions of the past."

American planner Albert Mayer made the first master plan, but when his colleague Nowicki died in a plane crash in 1950, Nehru turned to Swiss-French architect Le Corbusier, along with British architects Maxwell Fry and Jane Drew, and Le Corbusier's cousin Pierre Jeanneret. Together they would transform an empty plain at the Shivalik foothills into the most ambitious planned city of the 20th century.

"Chandigarh is the biggest opportunity of my life." — Le Corbusier, 1950

Le Corbusier conceived the city as a human body — the Capitol Complex as the head, Sector 17 as the heart, the university as the mind. His master plan divided the city into self-sufficient numbered sectors, each with its own market, school, park, and housing. 160km of cycle tracks were built in — an extraordinary provision in 1952 India.

Le Corbusier visited Chandigarh only briefly twice a year. Day-to-day supervision of everything — Capitol Complex, civic buildings, furniture, street lamps, manhole covers — fell to his cousin Pierre Jeanneret, who lived in Chandigarh from 1951 until 1965. Jeanneret is the unsung hero of the city: without him, it would simply not exist as it does today. His ashes, per his own wishes, were scattered in Sukhna Lake in 1967.

The Capitol Complex was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in July 2016 as part of the "Architectural Work of Le Corbusier — an Outstanding Contribution to the Modern Movement."

Le Corbusier
1887–1965 · Swiss-French · Master Planner
Born Charles-Édouard Jeanneret in Switzerland, he became the 20th century's most influential architect. He designed the Capitol Complex and Chandigarh's master plan — his largest and most ambitious realised project. He died in 1965, never seeing the city fully complete. Today his legacy is preserved at the Fondation Le Corbusier, Paris, and at the Capitol Complex Tourist Centre, Sector 1.
Pierre Jeanneret
1896–1967 · Swiss · Chief Architect of Chandigarh
Le Corbusier's cousin and the on-the-ground architect of Chandigarh. He supervised every building, designed the city's iconic teak-and-cane furniture (now fetching enormous sums at international auction), and gave 15 years of his life to the project. He is buried in spirit here — his ashes are in Sukhna Lake. His home, House No. 57, Sector 5, is now the Pierre Jeanneret Museum.
Pierre Jeanneret Museum
House No. 57, Sector 5 · Open as Museum since 2017
Jeanneret's home for 11 years, opened as a museum on 22 March 2017 (his 121st birth anniversary). Displays his furniture (chairs and tables now worth hundreds of thousands at Christie's and Sotheby's), personal correspondence, and architectural drawings. Three bedrooms available as heritage guest rooms. Entry ₹50 · Closed Mondays · 10am–5pm.

🏛 UNESCO World Heritage Site · Inscribed 17 July 2016

The Capitol Complex —
Chandigarh's Crown

On 17 July 2016, the Capitol Complex of Chandigarh was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the "Architectural Work of Le Corbusier — an Outstanding Contribution to the Modern Movement" — a transnational serial nomination encompassing 17 Le Corbusier works across 7 countries. It is the first and only UNESCO World Heritage Site in the Indian subcontinent dedicated to modernist architecture. The Capitol Complex stands in Sector 1, against the dramatic backdrop of the Shivalik Hills. Entry requires prior registration; tickets and guided tours can be booked at chandigarhtourism.gov.in.

⚖️
High Court of Punjab & Haryana
The first building completed (1955). Dramatic brise-soleil concrete fins and vivid primary-coloured panels. Original Pierre Jeanneret teak furniture in interiors. India's most architecturally significant court building.
🏛️
The Secretariat
At 254 metres, the largest building Le Corbusier ever designed. The ramps and brise-soleil facade create dramatic light effects. Houses the administrative machinery of Punjab and Haryana.
🗳️
Palace of Assembly
Legislative assembly of Punjab and Haryana. Features a hyperbolic cooling tower, pilotis, and a solar funnel flooding the chamber with daylight. One of the most theatrical political buildings in the world.
🤲
The Open Hand Monument
A 26-metre rotating steel hand — open to give and receive. Le Corbusier's defining symbol for the city. Completed 1985 (20 years after his death). The emblem of Chandigarh on all official seals.

Iconic Landmark · Sector 1
Rock Garden
Forty acres of labyrinthine sculptures, waterfalls, and folk figurines built secretly by Nek Chand Saini over 18 years from urban waste — broken bangles, ceramic tiles, steel pipes, electrical fittings. Chand, a government road inspector, began in 1957 and revealed his hidden kingdom to the world in 1976. The UT Administration, initially inclined to demolish it, instead designated it a public space. Today it receives 5,000+ visitors daily and is one of India's most visited sites.

Natural Landmark · Sector 1
Sukhna Lake
A man-made reservoir created in 1958 by damming the Sukhna Choe seasonal stream, at the foot of the Shivalik Hills. Its 3.5-km promenade is Chandigarh's most beloved social space — joggers, cyclists, lovers, and early-morning walkers converge here at dawn. In 2021 the lake and its surrounding catchment were designated a wildlife sanctuary, protecting 25 migratory bird species including bar-headed geese, common teals, and flamingos. The Morni Hills are visible on a clear winter day.

Mughal Heritage · 22 km from city · Haryana
Pinjore Heritage Gardens
Built in the 17th century by Nawab Fidai Khan Koka, foster-brother of Emperor Aurangzeb, and later renovated and expanded by Maharaja Yadavindra Singh of Patiala — hence also called Yadavindra Gardens. Modelled on Shalimar Bagh in Srinagar, its seven terraced levels descend through Mughal pavilions, fountains, water channels, and seasonal flower beds. The gardens also contain a Japanese Garden, Rose Garden, mini-zoo, heritage train, and an open-air museum. Illuminated in the evenings for a magical effect.

Historic Fort · Manimajra · Est. c.1660
Manimajra Fort
One of the oldest structures in the Chandigarh region, with a history of over 360 years. Built around 1660 CE, the fort became a seat of the Manimajra royal family and was later used by various Sikh and Punjabi rulers. It gained popular attention after being featured as a filming location in the Oscar-nominated Hollywood film Zero Dark Thirty (2012). The fort sits atop a hill with commanding views over the region and contains the Shahidi Qila Sahib Gurdwara within its premises — a shrine commemorating Sikh martyrs who fought against Mughal oppression. An atmospheric, undervisited heritage gem.

Asia's Largest Rose Garden · Sector 16
Zakir Hussain Rose Garden
Asia's largest rose garden — 30 acres, 1,600+ varieties, 50,000 rose plants named after India's third President. Established in 1967 under the vision of Dr. M.S. Randhawa, Chandigarh's first Chief Commissioner. Also contains 32,000 ornamental plants, medicinal plants, and 825 varieties of trees. In February/March, the annual Rose Festival transforms the garden into a 3-day city-wide carnival with live concerts, helicopter rides, floral exhibitions, and competitions. Host to the Rose Festival since 1961.

Tricity Heritage

Historic Mohali & Panchkula

Mohali (officially SAS Nagar — named for Sahibzada Ajit Singh, eldest son of Guru Gobind Singh) carries deep Sikh heritage. It was in this land that Banda Singh Bahadur fought the decisive Battle of Chappar Chiri (1710). The tricity's heritage sites range from war memorials to ancient Gurdwaras, from sacred temples to colonial cantonment towns.

War Memorial · Mohali
Baba Banda Singh Bahadur War Memorial
📍 Near Chappar Chiri, Mohali · ~20 km from Sector 17
Built to commemorate the Battle of Chappar Chiri (1710), where Banda Singh Bahadur defeated the Mughal forces of Wazir Khan — the first major Sikh military victory. The memorial features a grand monument, a lake, sculptures, and an informative museum documenting this pivotal chapter of Sikh history. Inaugurated in 2011.
From Sector 17: Take NH-5 West towards Mohali, exit at Phase 10, follow signs to Chappar Chiri · ~25 min drive · Entry ₹20
Sacred Heritage · Mohali
Gurdwara Amb Sahib
📍 Mohali, Phase 7 · ~15 km from Sector 17
One of the most historically significant Gurdwaras in Mohali, associated with Guru Gobind Singh's period. A tranquil place of worship with a serene sarovar (sacred pool). The langar (community kitchen) serves free meals to all visitors, irrespective of faith. Heads must be covered on entry — scarves available at the entrance.
From Sector 17: Take Madhya Marg west, enter Mohali via Phase 3B2, continue to Phase 7 · ~20 min · Free entry
Ancient Temple · Panchkula
Mata Mansa Devi Mandir
📍 Sector 5, Panchkula · ~10 km from Sector 17
One of the most prominent Shakti shrines in North India, at the foothills of the Shivalik range. The main shrine was built between 1811–1815 by Maharaja Gopal Das Singh of Mani Majra. The complex contains three ancient shrines. Lakhs of devotees visit all year. Especially crowded during Navratri when a grand fair is held.
From Sector 17: Take NH-5 East to Panchkula, turn onto Panchkula-Kalka Road towards Mansa Devi · ~15 min · Free entry
Sacred Shrine · Panchkula
Nada Sahib Gurdwara
📍 On River Ghaggar, Panchkula · ~14 km from Sector 17
One of the most revered Gurdwaras in the region, associated with Guru Gobind Singh's passage through the area. Located on the banks of the Ghaggar river, it draws tens of thousands of devotees weekly. The setting — water, forest, and mountains — is singularly beautiful. Langar served 24 hours a day.
From Sector 17: Take NH-5 East towards Panchkula, follow signs to Nada Sahib via Panchkula Industrial Area · ~20 min · Free
Ancient Fort · Chandigarh UT
Burail Fort
📍 Burail Village, Chandigarh · ~8 km from Sector 17
A Mughal-era fort that served as the stronghold of a Mughal general until 1712, when it was captured by the Khalsa Army under Banda Singh Bahadur following the general's oppressive conduct. Today it stands as a partly ruined but evocative monument, with the Shahidi Qila Sahib Gurdwara within its grounds — a shrine to the Sikh warriors who died in the battle.
From Sector 17: Take Tribune Chowk Road to Burail village · ~15 min · Free entry · Daylight hours only
Freedom Memorial · Mohali
Dr. Diwan Singh Kalepani Museum
📍 Near Siswan Dam, Mohali · ~15 km from Sector 17
A beautifully maintained museum dedicated to Dr. Diwan Singh Kalepani, eminent Punjabi writer and freedom fighter. Features life-size posters of independence leaders, graphic representation of historic events, and a replica of the prison cell at Cellular Jail, Port Blair. Located on the Siswan Dam road — combine with a visit to the Oberoi Sukhvilas or Siswan forest.
From Sector 17: Take NH-5 West, exit at Phase 3B2, follow Siswan Dam road · ~25 min · Small entry fee
Sikh Heritage · Fatehgarh Sahib
Fatehburj War Memorial
📍 Chappar Chiri, Near Mohali · ~20 km from Sector 17
Inaugurated in 2011, this monument commemorates the Battle of Chappar Chiri. A 100-foot-tall tower engraved with names and events of the battle. The surrounding complex includes a museum, reflecting pool, and landscaped gardens. Located near the Banda Singh Bahadur War Memorial — both can be visited together.
From Sector 17: NH-5 West towards Mohali, Chappar Chiri exit · ~25 min · Entry ₹20
Cantonment Town · Panchkula
Chandi Mandir Cantonment
📍 Panchkula, Haryana · ~12 km from Sector 17
The Chandigarh name derives from "Chandi-garh" — the fort of Chandi Goddess. The ancient Chandi Mata temple that gave the city its name stands here, in the Chandi Mandir cantonment area on the outskirts. An active military cantonment housing Western Command HQ, with the original Chandi temple that is the city's namesake. A place of deep religious and historical significance.
From Sector 17: Take NH-5 East towards Panchkula, turn at Chandi Mandir junction · ~18 min · Temple: free entry

Green Chandigarh

Gardens of Chandigarh

Le Corbusier's master plan reserved 40% of the city's land as green space. Over 43 parks and gardens thread through the sectors, anchored by the 8-km Leisure Valley. Chandigarh is India's greenest major city — and its most fragrant.

Culture & Knowledge

Museums of Chandigarh

Chandigarh's museums span 5,000 years — from Indus Valley artefacts and Gandhara Buddhist sculpture to Le Corbusier's original architectural drawings. Most are clustered in the Sector 10 museum campus.

01
Flagship · Le Corbusier Building · Sector 10C
Government Museum & Art Gallery
Inaugurated 1968 in a Le Corbusier-designed building. One of the world's largest Gandhara sculpture collections (627 pieces), Pahari and Rajasthani miniature paintings, ancient sculpture, and Indian contemporary art. 🕙 10am–4:40pm · Closed Mon · 🎟 ₹20
02
Architecture · Sector 10C
Chandigarh Architecture Museum
Rare master plans, drawings and sketches by Le Corbusier, Pierre Jeanneret, Jane Drew, Maxwell Fry, and Albert Mayer. Models of two Capitol Complex buildings never built. Included with Government Museum ticket. 🕙 10am–4:40pm · Closed Mon
03

Le Corbusier's original architect's office — now a museum (2008). Photographs, furniture, paintings, and documents on the conception of Chandigarh. Souvenir shop on-site. 🕙 10am–5pm · Closed Mon · 🎟 Free
04

Pierre Jeanneret Museum
Jeanneret's home for 11 years — House No. 57, Sector 5. Opened 2017. Displays his iconic teak-and-cane furniture (now fetching enormous sums at global auctions). Three bedrooms available as heritage guest rooms. 🕙 10am–5pm · Closed Mon · 🎟 ₹50

Calendar & Culture

Festivals & Events

Plan your visit around Chandigarh's festivals — from the city's beloved Rose Festival (rose tickets at chandigarhtourism.gov.in) to Lohri bonfires and the Chandigarh Carnival.

February / March
Rose Festival ✦ Flagship
3 days at Zakir Hussain Rose Garden. 1,600+ rose varieties, photography competitions, amusement park, food stalls, and three Mega Musical Nights. 54th edition held Feb 2026. Pre-book at chandigarhtourism.gov.in. Helicopter rides also offered during the festival.
December
Chrysanthemum Show
Annual winter flower festival at Terraced Garden, Sector 33. Thousands of chrysanthemum varieties, flower animal figures, rangoli, and cultural entertainment.
February
Bougainvillea Festival
Annual festival at Bougainvillea Garden, Sector 3. Vivid pinks, purples, and reds in full bloom. Cultural programmes and evening events — often combined with the Rose Festival for a flower weekend.
January
Lohri
Punjabi harvest festival — bonfires at dusk, rewri, til and popcorn tossed into the flames, songs of Dulla Bhatti. Most alive in residential sectors like 22 and 35.
March
Holi
Festival of colours with Punjabi exuberance. Public celebrations at Sukhna Lake promenade and Sector 17 plaza. Hotels host ticketed Holi events with live music.
November
Guru Nanak Jayanti
Gurdwaras host Akhand Paths and kirtan through the night. Nagar Kirtan processions move through the sectors. An atmosphere of deep peace and community.
October
Chandigarh Carnival
Annual cultural festival along the Leisure Valley — folk performances, classical music, art installations, street food, photography exhibitions. Check UT Tourism portal for programme.
March–April
Baisakhi
Punjabi New Year and harvest festival — bhangra, giddha, fairs, community meals. Sector 17 plaza and Gurdwaras host the largest gatherings.

Adventure & Fun

Things to Do in & Around Chandigarh

Chandigarh surprises most visitors with its adventure credentials. The Shivalik Hills begin at the city's northern edge — paragliding, trekking, river rafting, and forest camping are within an hour's reach. Within the city, cycling, water sports, go-karting, and escape rooms offer day-round entertainment.

🪂
Paragliding
Morni Hills (45 km) · Kasauli (58 km) · Shivalik Hills
Gentle slopes and favourable wind conditions make the Shivaliks ideal for beginners and experienced gliders alike. Morni Hills is the nearest site. Certified operators provide tandem flights with full safety briefings. The views of the Shivalik range are extraordinary.
Oct–June₹2,500–3,500 tandemBeginners welcome
🚵
Cycling
City-wide · 160km dedicated tracks
160km of dedicated cycle tracks make Chandigarh one of India's best cycling cities. Rent from Sukhna Lake or your hotel. The Leisure Valley route, Rock Garden circuit, and the Rose Garden loop are the most scenic. Available year-round.
Year-round₹100–200 rental/dayAll levels
🥾
Trekking
Morni Hills · Kasauli · Nepli Forest · Kansal Reserve
Six official trekking routes maintained by the Chandigarh Administration. Morni Hills and Kasauli offer spectacular forest trails. The UT Administration charges a small fee for Sukhna Wildlife Sanctuary trek. Guided treks available through operators in Sector 22.
Oct–March best₹50 entry SukhnaBeginner to moderate

Traveller's Essentials
  • Capitol Complex requires prior online registration. Book at chandigarhtourism.gov.in — walk-in access is limited and subject to security clearance.
  • Rose Festival tickets (Mega Musical Nights) also available at chandigarhtourism.gov.in — book early as these sell out.
  • Rock Garden opens at 9am. Arrive early on weekdays to avoid school groups.
  • Most sector markets close on Monday. Sector 17 is the main exception and stays open all week.
  • Chandigarh has a strict no-plastic policy. Littering carries real fines — visitors are expected to respect it.
  • Alcohol is fully licensed in Chandigarh, unlike parts of neighbouring Punjab. Many restaurants have full bar service.
  • December–January nights can be cold (5–8°C). Pack warm layers for winter visits.
  • ATMs are plentiful. UPI and cards widely accepted in Sectors 17, 35, and the Elante Mall area.
  • At Anandpur Sahib and Virasat-e-Khalsa, cover your head before entering. Scarves are available at the entrance free of charge.
  • The Pierre Jeanneret Museum (Sector 5) is deeply undervisited — a hidden gem for architecture and design enthusiasts.
